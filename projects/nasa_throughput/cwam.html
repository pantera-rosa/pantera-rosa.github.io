Convective Weather Avoidance Model (CWAM) Data

Context

CWAM (Convective Weather Avoidance Model) data provide forecast on the potential impact of convective weather. For each time of prediction, the data is a list of polygons at a specific altitude within which there is a percentage of chance greater than 60, 70, 80% to be impacted by convective weather. Polygons are represented by a list of their vertices in latitude, longitude (degrees). Forecasts are produced in general every 15 minutes and each forecast is made up to 2 hours in the future with a 5-minute interval. CWAM data are stored as compressed HDF5 files (.h5) with BZIP2 compression (.bz2).

This type of data is extremely important to our model's predictions. Convection produces conditions that impact flight conditions - lightning, heavy rain, icing, hail, and turbulence - and is the number 1 reason for delays in the U.S. National Airspace System.

Findings

As expected, convective activity is more frequent at flight level 250, which is approximately 25,000 feet. Convection requires both moisture and instability, and both of these characteristics are more common at lower altitudes, since dryness and coldness increases and altitude increases. This is also where you are most likely to experience turbulence.

In the figure above, you can actually see the convective weather activity per hour, and note how much more frequent convective weather activity becomes in the evenings. This is a known phenomena with events like thunderstorms.

In this visual, a high concentration of activity is evident near Florida. Florida, with its hot, humid weather, peninsular shape, solar heating, jet stream influence, and general instability, is a prime spot for convective weather activity, and the data visualization aligns with what is expected.
